## Project Overview

For this project, I will explore how behavioural data can be transformed into emotional and atmospheric experiences rather than being presented through traditional charts, graphs, or numerical visualisations.

The project is situated within a speculative near-future scenario where digital systems continuously collect behavioural information and construct interpretations of users without directly asking for personal input. Instead of treating data as objective facts, the project investigates how systems translate behavioural patterns into emotional assumptions, identity profiles, and personalised experiences.

The working title for the project is **ECHO//PROFILE**.

### Design Question

**How might we visualise the ways digital systems interpret and construct personal identity through behavioural data?**

### Project Aim

The aim of this project is to create a data-driven interactive visualisation that reveals how behavioural data can be transformed into algorithmic interpretations of identity.

Rather than displaying data through charts and metrics, the project will visualise data through atmosphere, environmental transformation, generative systems, and personalised profile generation.

The project seeks to encourage reflection on:

* Data interpretation
* Algorithmic profiling
* Digital identity
* Self-perception
* Data Humanism

# Data Exploration

## Data Source Audit

Unlike traditional data visualisation projects that rely on existing datasets, this project will generate its own dataset through user interaction.

The primary dataset will consist of behavioural information collected while users interact with an exploratory environment.

### Data Source

Examples include:

* Distance travelled
* Movement speed
* Mouse clicks

### Limitations and Biases

A major limitation of this dataset is that behavioural information cannot directly represent internal emotional states.

The system can only observe actions rather than thoughts, motivations, or intentions.

For example, a user remaining stationary could indicate calmness, boredom, confusion, or distraction. The data alone cannot determine which interpretation is correct.

As a result, the emotional and personality profiles generated by the project should be understood as interpretations rather than objective truths.

This limitation becomes a central conceptual component of the project and directly relates to broader questions about algorithmic profiling and data representation.

# Independent Study

## Consultation Reflection

During the proposal consultation, I discussed my interest in emotional visualisation and interactive data systems. The conversation helped clarify that the strongest aspect of the project was not the emotional atmosphere itself but the invisible process occurring behind it.

Rather than simply visualising emotions, I began focusing on how systems interpret behaviour and construct assumptions about users. This shifted the project towards questions of algorithmic profiling, identity construction, and data representation.

The consultation also highlighted the importance of making the interpretation process visible. Instead of only showing visual outputs, I plan to reveal how behavioural information is transformed into generated profiles. This change strengthens the project's critical engagement with Data Humanism and moves it beyond a purely aesthetic visualisation.

As a result, I refined the project into a system that tracks behaviour, generates interpretations, and encourages users to reflect on how digital systems construct identities through data.

# Reflection

This week helped me move from a broad research direction towards a clearer project framework. While my original proposal focused on emotional visualisation, I realised that the most compelling aspect of the project lies in how systems interpret people through data. This shift significantly strengthened the conceptual foundation of the project and aligned more closely with ideas discussed in Data Humanism.

Through data exploration and precedent research, I began to see data as an active participant in shaping experiences rather than a neutral record of behaviour. This challenged my previous assumption that data visualisation should focus primarily on representation. Instead, I became interested in how data can influence perception, identity, and self-understanding.

The consultation reinforced the importance of critical engagement with data rather than simply creating a visually appealing output. As a result, the project evolved from an emotional visualisation system into an exploration of algorithmic interpretation and identity construction.

Technically, this week highlighted the importance of building the project incrementally. Rather than focusing on visual polish, I recognised that behaviour tracking and interpretation systems will form the conceptual core of the project. Establishing these systems early will provide a strong foundation for future experiments involving atmosphere generation, profile construction, and reflection.

Moving forward, I aim to test how behavioural data can be collected and transformed into meaningful interpretations while maintaining a critical perspective on algorithmic profiling and digital identity.
